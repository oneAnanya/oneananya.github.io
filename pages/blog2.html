Database Normalization

A comprehensive guide to organizing data efficiently and eliminating redundancy through systematic normalization forms

Unnormalized Form (UNF)

Initially, data may be unorganized with repeating groups and no clear structure. This is the starting point before any normalization process.

Key Issue: Data redundancy and potential inconsistencies make maintenance difficult.

First Normal Form 1NF

1NF ensures each column contains atomic (indivisible) values, and each record is unique. No repeating groups are allowed.

Requirements: Atomic values, unique rows, and a primary key to identify each record.

Second Normal Form 2NF

2NF removes partial dependencies. All non-key columns must depend on the entire primary key, not just part of it. Since our table has a single-column primary key (Student ID), it's already in 2NF.

Key Concept: Eliminates partial dependencies on composite primary keys.

Third Normal Form 3NF

3NF removes transitive dependencies. Non-key columns must not depend on other non-key columns. We split our table to eliminate these dependencies.

Boyce-Codd Normal Form BCNF

BCNF is a stricter version of 3NF that ensures no non-trivial functional dependencies of attributes on anything other than a superkey. Our current tables already comply with BCNF.

Enhanced Rule: Every determinant must be a candidate key.

Fourth Normal Form 4NF

4NF deals with multi-valued dependencies. A table is in 4NF if it's in BCNF and has no multi-valued dependencies.

Fifth Normal Form 5NF

5NF deals with join dependencies. A table is in 5NF if it's in 4NF and cannot be decomposed into smaller tables without losing data.
